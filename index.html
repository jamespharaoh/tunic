Tunic alphabet

This page contains my notes from deciphering the alphabet used in the game Tunic. Please close the page if you haven't deciphered it yet and you don't want any spoilers!

Consonants

m p tʃ y r f k s t θ ʃ n
w b dʒ l h v g z d ð ʒ ŋ

Vowels

ə/ʌ ɔ a u ɔr εər oʊ eɪ aɪ
ɪ ʊ ε i ər ɪər ɑr ɔɪ aʊ

Notes

  • I currently show the words without the gap in the middle used in the game. I see this as a stylistic difference and that I am simply using a different, more basic font. In the future I might implement different fonts.

Vowels

  • What is shown as "a" here actually covers several sounds, including "æ" and "ɑ". Usage of these sounds also varies a lot based on accent; for example "can't" would typically be pronounced with "ɑ" or "a" in the UK and "æ" in the United States. For simplicity, I just use the latin "a".
  • Many words are spelt with "ʊ" in the game, often after a "d" and before an "l" or "n", where dictionaries would generally use an "ə" or omit the phoneme entirely. I actually think if you omit the vowel it sounds like a very short "ʊ" because of the mouth position required to pronounce the consonant. (Note that when I say they are spelt with "ʊ" I mean that it is written the same way as, for example, the vowel in "look", so I assume this is the intended sound.)
  • There are a few other words with unusual vowel sounds, for example "swamp". I have not list these anywhere.

Native speaker versions

There are a number of words which are spelt in a way which doesn't seem to match the pronunciation found in the dictionary. Where this is the case, and if I think the pronunciation would be extremely unusual for a native speaker, then I have marked these words with "as written in game", and also included my own version marked with "native pronunciation". Most of these are instances of the following:

  • There are many examples of "ɪ", often before an "n", where the dictionary and most native speakers would omit the sound or use a neutral sound like "ə".
  • There are several words spelt with a long "i" sound where the dictionary or a native speaker would use a short "ɪ", often in words which start with the letter "e". The "i" sound is generally understood to mean the same thing, and may even be used by a native speaker for emphasis, but I don't think it is how these words would be spelt phonetically in a printed document.
